São Paulo's Subway Expanded,
Workers Went Elsewhere

By Giovanna Serafim

São Paulo has poured billions into expanding its subway network over the past two decades, stretching its lines farther into the city’s periphery in an effort to keep up with Latin America’s largest urban economy. More stations, more lines, more coverage — all aimed at helping people get to work faster and more efficiently.

But as the city’s subway map has grown, so too have the challenges. Construction delays, funding constraints, and complex land rights issues have slowed progress on key projects. At the same time, ridership has surged, putting pressure on existing infrastructure and raising concerns about overcrowding and long-term capacity.

For residents of São Paulo’s outer districts, the stakes are high. In neighborhoods where daily commutes can stretch beyond two hours each way, access to a nearby metro station can mean a radical shift in quality of life — unlocking job opportunities, shortening travel times, and offering a safer, more reliable alternative to crowded buses or informal transport options.

Between 2007 and 2023, São Paulo’s job landscape underwent a major transformation. As the subway network expanded, companies followed, gradually migrating toward areas with better transit access. Traditional business hubs like Avenida Paulista remained strong, while regions like Faria Lima saw a surge in finance, tech, and creative industries. Meanwhile, parts of the city’s center began attracting companies back with lower rents and improved infrastructure.

In recent years, a growing number of companies have been returning to São Paulo’s central regions. From fintech giants like Nubank setting up on Avenida Paulista to banks, architecture firms, and creative studios moving downtown, the city’s business core is being reactivated. Lower rental costs and better subway access are helping drive this shift.
